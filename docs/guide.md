# Life in the UK Guide

## Acknowledgement

This app is an independent revision resource based on *Life in the United Kingdom: A Guide for New Residents*. The study notes have been rewritten in original wording and are provided to help users prepare for the Life in the UK Test.

This app is not an official government product and is not approved, sponsored, reviewed, or endorsed by the Home Office, GOV.UK, or any UK government body.

## 1. The values and principles of the UK

There is no place in UK society for extremism or intolerance.

### Fundamental principles to remember

- **Democracy** — people have a role in choosing the government.
- **Rule of law** — everyone is expected to follow the law.
- **Individual liberty** — people have personal freedoms and choices.
- **Mutual tolerance** — people should respect those with different religions, beliefs, and opinions.
- **Community participation** — residents are encouraged to contribute to local life.

### Citizenship ceremony pledge — meaning

New citizens make a promise to:

- be loyal to the UK
- respect UK rights and freedoms
- support democratic values
- obey the law
- carry out the responsibilities of citizenship

### Responsibilities of residents and citizens

People living permanently in the UK are expected to:

- follow the law
- respect other people’s rights and views
- treat people fairly
- care for themselves and their family
- help look after their local area and the environment

### Freedoms and rights in the UK

The UK protects important freedoms and legal rights, including:

- freedom to follow a religion or belief
- freedom to express opinions
- protection from unfair discrimination
- access to a fair trial
- the ability to take part in choosing the government

### Becoming a permanent resident or citizen

Applicants normally need to show:

- knowledge of life in the UK
- knowledge of English

This usually means:

- passing the Life in the UK Test
- proving English ability through an accepted route

Always check GOV.UK before applying, because rules can change.

### Life in the UK Test basics

- The test is based on the official *Guide for New Residents*.
- Questions can come from the whole handbook, including the introduction.
- You do **not** need to memorise dates of birth or death.
- The test has **24 questions**.
- The test is normally in English, but Welsh or Scottish Gaelic may be possible.
- It must be taken at an approved test centre.
- Booking should be done through the official GOV.UK route.
- Bring the correct original ID used for booking; otherwise, you may not be allowed to take the test.

## 2. What is the UK?

### Countries of the UK

The UK consists of:

- **England**
- **Scotland**
- **Wales**
- **Northern Ireland**

### Important name differences

- **Official country name:** United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland.
- **Great Britain:** England, Scotland, and Wales only.
- **Northern Ireland:** part of the UK, but not part of Great Britain.
- **Ireland:** the rest of the island of Ireland is a separate independent country.

### Related places that are not part of the UK

#### Crown dependencies

These have their own governments:

- **Channel Islands**
- **Isle of Man**

#### British overseas territories

These are connected to the UK but are not part of it. Examples include:

- **St Helena**
- **Falkland Islands**

### Government structure

- The UK Parliament is based at **Westminster**.
- Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland also have their own parliament or assembly.
- These national bodies have **devolved powers**, meaning they can make decisions in certain areas.

# A long and illustrious history

## 3. Britain since 1945

### Welfare state after the war

- In **1945**, the Labour Party won the general election and **Clement Attlee** became Prime Minister.
- The **NHS** was created in **1948**; **Aneurin "Nye" Bevan** played a leading role.
- Major industries were nationalised, including railways, coal, gas, water, and electricity.

### Key people: welfare and education

| Person | Role |
|---|---|
| **Clement Attlee** | Labour Prime Minister 1945-1951; created the NHS and expanded the welfare state |
| **William Beveridge** | Economist; his **1942 report** influenced the welfare state |
| **R. A. Butler** | Conservative politician; **Education Act 1944** introduced free secondary education |

### End of Empire and international role

- In **1947**, **India**, **Pakistan**, and **Ceylon** became independent.
- The UK developed its own atomic bomb and joined **NATO**.
- **Harold Macmillan** is associated with the "wind of change" speech about decolonisation.

### Migration in post-war Britain

- In **1948**, people from the West Indies were invited to work in Britain.
- During the 1950s, workers came from the West Indies, India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh.

### Social change in the 1960s

- The 1960s are called the **"Swinging Sixties"**.
- Important bands: **The Beatles** and **The Rolling Stones**.
- Britain and France developed **Concorde**.
- Laws became more liberal in divorce and abortion.

### British inventions and discoveries of the 20th century

| Invention / discovery | Key person |
|---|---|

| Radar | **Sir Robert Watson-Watt** |
| Turing machine | **Alan Turing** |

### Economy and unrest in the 1970s

- The post-war economic boom ended in the late 1970s.
- Prices rose sharply and the pound was unstable.
- Strikes affected many industries; tensions grew between trade unions and government.

### The Thatcher government (1979-1990)

- In **1979**, **Margaret Thatcher** became the UK's first female Prime Minister.
- Her government introduced **Thatcherism**: market forces, privatisation, and reduced trade union power.

### The Blair government and beyond

- In **1997**, **Tony Blair** led Labour to victory.
- His government created the Scottish Parliament and Welsh Assembly.
- The **Good Friday Agreement** (1998) brought peace to Northern Ireland.
- The **Human Rights Act** (1998) made the European Convention on Human Rights part of UK law.
- The **National Minimum Wage** and civil partnerships were introduced.

### Coalition government (2010-2015)

- In **2010**, the Conservative and Liberal Democrat parties formed the first coalition government since 1945.

### Brexit

- In **2016**, the UK voted to leave the European Union in a referendum.
- The UK formally left the EU on **31 January 2020**.

## 4. Early Britain

### Early settlers

- The first people to live in Britain arrived over **10,000 years ago**.
- **Stonehenge** was built about **5,000 years ago**.
- **Skara Brae** in Orkney, Scotland, is the best preserved prehistoric village in northern Europe.

### The Romans

- The Romans ruled Britain from **AD 43 to AD 410**.
- They built roads, public baths, and fortified towns.
- **Hadrian's Wall** was built to keep out tribes from Scotland.
- The Romans remained in Britain for **400 years**.

### The Anglo-Saxons

- After the Romans left, **Anglo-Saxon** kingdoms were formed in England.
- **St Augustine** led missionaries from Rome in **597** and became the first Archbishop of Canterbury.
- Anglo-Saxons were skilled craftsmen and traders.

### The Viking era

- Vikings came from **Denmark, Norway, and Sweden**.
- They raided Britain but also traded and settled.
- **King Alfred the Great** of Wessex defeated the Vikings and is remembered for promoting education and justice.

### The Norman Conquest and the Domesday Book

- **William the Conqueror** defeated **King Harold** at the **Battle of Hastings** in **1066**.
- William sent officials to collect information for the **Domesday Book**.
- The Domesday Book recorded towns, villages, landowners, and residents.

## 5. The 20th century

### The First World War

- The war began in **1914** and ended on **11 November 1918**.
- Britain fought with the Allied Powers, including France, Russia, and later the USA.
- Over **2 million** British and Commonwealth troops died.

### The partition of Ireland

- In **1922**, Ireland was divided into two parts.
- **Northern Ireland** remained part of the UK.
- The **Irish Free State** (now the Republic of Ireland) became independent.

### The inter-war period

- The **1920s** and **1930s** were difficult for many people.
- The **Great Depression** caused unemployment, especially in traditional industries.
- The **BBC** began radio broadcasting in **1922** and regular television in **1936**.
- **Sir John Logie Baird** made the first television broadcasts.

### The Second World War

- Britain declared war on Germany in **1939** after the invasion of Poland.
- **Winston Churchill** became Prime Minister in **1940** and led Britain during the war.
- Important events:
  - **Dunkirk** (1940): British and Allied soldiers were evacuated from France
  - **Battle of Britain** (1940): The Royal Air Force defended the UK against German air attacks
  - **The Blitz**: German bombing of British cities
  - **D-Day** (1944): Allied invasion of France
- Germany was defeated in **May 1945**.
- The war against Japan ended in **August 1945** after the USA dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki.

### Winston Churchill

- **Winston Churchill** was Prime Minister during the Second World War.
- He is famous for his speeches, including "we shall fight on the beaches".
- He was also an artist, historian, and writer, winning the **Nobel Prize in Literature** in **1953**.

### Alexander Fleming and penicillin

- **Alexander Fleming** discovered **penicillin** in **1928** at St Mary's Hospital in London.
- **Howard Florey** and **Ernst Chain** helped develop it into a usable medicine.
- Fleming won the Nobel Prize in Medicine in **1945**.

## 6. A global power

### Constitutional monarchy and the Bill of Rights

- The **Bill of Rights 1689** limited royal power and confirmed Parliament's rights.
- The monarch had to be **Protestant**; Parliament had to be elected regularly.
- Two early groups developed: **Whigs** and **Tories**.
- From **1695**, newspapers no longer needed a government licence.
- This system became known as **constitutional monarchy**.

### Voting at this time

- Voting was limited to property-owning men; women could not vote.
- Some seats were unfair: **pocket boroughs** (controlled by wealthy families) and **rotten boroughs** (very few voters).

### Population movement and refugees

- Jews returned to settle in London from **1656**.
- Between **1680 and 1720**, many **Huguenots** (French Protestant refugees) came to Britain.

### Act of Union, 1707

- The **Act of Union 1707** joined England, Wales, and Scotland into the **Kingdom of Great Britain**.
- Scotland kept its own legal system, education system, and Presbyterian Church.

### The first Prime Minister

- **Sir Robert Walpole** is regarded as the first Prime Minister, serving from **1721 to 1742**.

### Jacobite rebellion and Highland Clearances

- In **1745**, **Bonnie Prince Charlie** tried to restore the Stuart line but was defeated at **Culloden** (1746).
- The **Highland Clearances** followed: small farms (**crofts**) were cleared for sheep and cattle.

### The Enlightenment

- The **Enlightenment** was an 18th-century period of new ideas.
- Leading Scottish thinkers: **Adam Smith** (economics), **David Hume** (philosophy), **James Watt** (steam power).

### Industrial Revolution

- The **Industrial Revolution** was the rapid growth of mechanised industry in the 18th and 19th centuries.
- Britain was the first country to industrialise on a large scale.
- Causes included new machinery, steam power, and demand for coal.
- The **Bessemer process** made mass steel production possible.
- Canals, shipbuilding, and railways expanded rapidly.
- Working conditions were often dangerous, with long hours and little protection.

### Colonies, trade, and global expansion

- **Captain James Cook** mapped Australia; colonies were established there.
- Britain gained control over Canada and large parts of India via the **East India Company**.
- Britain imported sugar and tobacco from North America, textiles and tea from India.

### Slave trade and abolition

- **Quakers** formed early anti-slavery groups.
- **William Wilberforce** helped lead the campaign in Parliament.
- **1807:** slave trading from British ships became illegal.
- **1833:** slavery was abolished across the British Empire.

### American War of Independence

- Colonists objected to British taxes because they had no representation in Parliament.
- In **1776**, 13 colonies declared independence; Britain recognised this in **1783**.

### Wars with France

- **1805:** Britain defeated French and Spanish fleets at the **Battle of Trafalgar**.
- **Admiral Nelson** led the fleet and died; **Nelson's Column** commemorates him.
- **1815:** Napoleon was defeated by the **Duke of Wellington** at the **Battle of Waterloo**.

### Act of Union, 1800 and the Union Flag

- The **Act of Union 1800** created the **United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland** in **1801**.
- The **Union Flag** combines **St George's Cross** (England), **St Andrew's Cross** (Scotland), and **St Patrick's Cross** (Ireland).
- The Welsh dragon is not included because Wales was already united with England.

### Victorian Age

- **Queen Victoria** reigned from **1837 to 1901** (the **Victorian Age**).
- Britain became more powerful overseas; the middle classes grew more important.
- The British Empire became the largest in history, with over **400 million** people.
- Between **1853 and 1913**, about **13 million** people left Britain.

### Trade and industry in the Victorian period

- Britain promoted **free trade**; the **Corn Laws** were repealed in **1846**.
- In **1847**, women and children's factory work was limited to **10 hours per day**.
- **George and Robert Stephenson** helped pioneer the railway engine.
- Britain produced more than half of the world's iron, coal, and cotton cloth.
- The **Great Exhibition** opened in **1851** in the Crystal Palace in Hyde Park.

### Crimean War

- The **Crimean War** lasted from **1853 to 1856**.
- Queen Victoria introduced the **Victoria Cross** for exceptional bravery.

### Ireland in the 19th century

- In the mid-19th century, the potato crop failed.
- Around **1 million people** died; about **1.5 million** emigrated.
- Irish nationalism grew. The **Fenians** wanted independence.
- **Charles Stuart Parnell** supported Home Rule.

### The right to vote

- The **Reform Act 1832** increased voters and removed pocket and rotten boroughs.
- The **Chartists** campaigned for working-class men's voting rights.
- The **Reform Act 1867** gave more urban men the vote.
- Before **1870**, a married woman's property belonged to her husband.
- Laws in **1870** and **1882** allowed married women to keep their own earnings and property.
- Campaigners for women's votes became known as **suffragettes**.
- In **1918**, women over 30 gained the right to vote.
- In **1928**, women gained voting rights on the same terms as men.

### Future of the Empire

- The **Boer War** (1899-1902) increased debate about the Empire's future.
- In the second half of the 20th century, many colonies became independent and joined the **Commonwealth**.

### Key people of the period

| Person | Role | Known for |
|---|---|---|
| **Robert Burns** | Scottish poet ("The Bard") | *Auld Lang Syne*, sung at Hogmanay |
| **Richard Arkwright** | Industrialist | Developed spinning mills and factory production |
| **Sake Dean Mahomet** | Entrepreneur from Bengal | Britain's first curry house (1810) |
| **Isambard Kingdom Brunel** | Engineer | Great Western Railway, bridges, ships |
| **Florence Nightingale** | Nurse | Founder of modern nursing; Crimean War hospitals |
| **Emmeline Pankhurst** | Suffragette | WSPU founder; campaigned for women's votes |
| **Rudyard Kipling** | Writer | Nobel Prize 1907; *The Jungle Book*, *If-* |

## 7. The Middle Ages

### Wars within Britain and Ireland

- Medieval kings of England fought to extend their power over Wales, Scotland, and Ireland.
- In **Wales**, English control became stronger.
- ****1284****: **Edward I** introduced the **Statute of Rhuddlan**, bringing Wales under the English Crown.
- Large castles such as **Conwy** and **Caernarfon** were built to secure English rule.
- By the mid-15th century, the final Welsh rebellions had been defeated.
- English law and the English language were introduced in Wales.

### Scotland in the Middle Ages

- English kings were less successful in Scotland.
- ****1314****: **Robert the Bruce** led the Scots to victory at the **Battle of Bannockburn**.
- Scotland was not conquered by England during this period.

### Ireland in the Middle Ages

- By around **1200**, the English controlled an area near Dublin called **the Pale**.

### Wars abroad

- Many knights joined the **Crusades**, wars in which European Christians fought for control of the Holy Land.
- English kings also fought France in the **Hundred Years’ War**.
- The Hundred Years’ War lasted **116 years**, despite its name.
- A famous battle was the **Battle of Agincourt** in **1415**.
- At Agincourt, **Henry V** defeated a much larger French army.
- The English had mostly left France by the **1450s**.

### Feudalism and land ownership

- The Normans used a system called **feudalism**.
- The king granted land to nobles in exchange for military support.
- Landowners had to provide soldiers for the king’s army.
- Some peasants owned land, but many were **serfs**.
- Serfs could farm a small part of their lord’s land but had to work for the lord and could not freely leave.
- A similar system developed in southern Scotland.
- In northern Scotland and Ireland, land was usually linked to **clans**, meaning powerful family groups.

### The Black Death

- ****1348****: a deadly disease, probably plague, reached Britain.
- It became known as the **Black Death**.
- About one third of people in England died.
- Scotland and Wales suffered similar losses.
- The Black Death was one of the greatest disasters in British history.

### Effects of the Black Death

- Labour became scarce; peasants could ask for higher wages.
- A stronger middle class developed in towns.

### Legal and political changes

- Medieval Parliament developed from the king’s council of advisers.
- The king’s council included nobles and senior Church leaders.
- ****1215****: King John was forced by powerful nobles to accept the **Magna Carta**.
- Magna Carta means **Great Charter**.
- It established the principle that the king was also under the law.
- It protected noble rights and limited the king’s ability to raise taxes or change laws without involving nobles.

### Development of Parliament

- Parliament divided into the **House of Lords** (nobles and bishops) and the **House of Commons** (knights and wealthy town representatives).
- Scotland developed its own Parliament with three groups, called **Estates**:
  - lords
  - commons
  - clergy

### Development of the legal system

- The idea that judges should be independent of government began to grow.
- In England, judges developed **common law**, based on past decisions and legal tradition.
- In Scotland, law developed differently and was more often written down in formal codes.

### English language and identity

- After the Norman Conquest, rulers spoke Norman French, while many ordinary people spoke Anglo-Saxon.
- Over time, these languages mixed and helped create modern English.
- By around **1400**, English was used in official documents and had become the main language of the royal court and Parliament.

### Literature and printing

- **Geoffrey Chaucer** wrote *The Canterbury Tales* in English before 1400.
- The work tells stories linked to a group of pilgrims travelling to Canterbury.
- It became one of the early books printed in England by **William Caxton**.
- Caxton was the first person in England to print books using a printing press.
- In Scotland, Gaelic continued to be spoken, and the Scots language developed.
- **John Barbour** wrote *The Bruce*, a poem about Robert the Bruce and the Battle of Bannockburn.

### Buildings and trade

- Castles were built across Britain and Ireland for defence.
- Some castles, such as **Windsor** and **Edinburgh**, are still used today.
- Large cathedrals were also built, including **Lincoln Cathedral**.
- Many cathedrals used stained glass to teach Bible stories and stories of saints.
- **York Minster** is famous for its stained glass.
- England became an important trading country.
- Wool was a major export.
- Skilled workers came from other countries, including:
  - weavers from France
  - engineers from Germany
  - glass makers from Italy
  - canal builders from Holland

### Wars of the Roses

- ****1455****: a civil war began over who should rule England.
- The conflict was between:
  - **House of Lancaster** — symbol: red rose
  - **House of York** — symbol: white rose
- The war is called the **Wars of the Roses**.
- It ended at the **Battle of Bosworth Field** in **1485**.
- **Richard III**, from the House of York, was killed.
- **Henry Tudor**, from the House of Lancaster, became **Henry VII**.
- Henry VII married **Elizabeth of York**, joining the two rival families.
- He became the first Tudor king.
- The Tudor symbol combined the red and white roses.

### Key things to remember



- Edward I brought Wales under the English Crown.
- Robert the Bruce defeated England at Bannockburn.
- The Pale was the area around Dublin controlled by the English.
- Feudalism linked landholding to military service.
- The Black Death killed about one third of the population and changed society.
- Magna Carta introduced the idea that even the king must obey the law.

## 8. The Tudors and Stuarts

### Henry VII and stronger monarchy

- **Henry VII** became king after the Wars of the Roses.
- He strengthened central government and reduced the power of the nobles.

### Henry VIII and the break with Rome

- **Henry VIII** separated England from the Pope and created the **Church of England**.
- Henry wanted to divorce **Catherine of Aragon**; the Pope refused.
- In the Church of England, the monarch, not the Pope, had authority.

### The six wives of Henry VIII

| Wife | Key points |
|---|---|
| **Catherine of Aragon** | Spanish princess; mother of Mary; Henry divorced her |
| **Anne Boleyn** | Mother of Elizabeth; executed at the Tower of London |
| **Jane Seymour** | Mother of Edward; died soon after his birth |
| **Anne of Cleves** | German princess; marriage arranged for politics; soon divorced |
| **Catherine Howard** | Cousin of Anne Boleyn; executed |
| **Catherine Parr** | Henry's last wife; survived him |

### The Reformation

- The **Reformation** challenged the Pope and the Roman Catholic Church.
- Protestant beliefs included reading the Bible in local languages and a direct relationship with God.
- Protestant ideas became stronger in England, Wales, and Scotland during the 16th century.

### Wales under Henry VIII

- Wales was formally joined with England by the **Act for the Government of Wales**.
- Wales began sending representatives to the House of Commons.

### Edward VI and Mary I

- **Edward VI** was strongly Protestant; the **Book of Common Prayer** was written during his reign.
- **Mary I** was Catholic and persecuted Protestants, earning the nickname **"Bloody Mary"**.

### Elizabeth I

- **Elizabeth I** restored the Church of England and balanced Catholic and Protestant views.
- She became especially popular after defeating the **Spanish Armada** in **1588**.
- The Armada was sent by Spain to restore Catholicism.

### Reformation in Scotland and Mary, Queen of Scots

- In **1560**, the Scottish Parliament ended the Pope's authority.
- The Protestant **Church of Scotland** was created with elected leadership; unlike the Church of England, it was not a state Church.
- **Mary, Queen of Scots** was Catholic. She gave the Scottish throne to her son **James VI**.
- Elizabeth I kept Mary prisoner; Mary was eventually executed for plotting against Elizabeth.

### Exploration and Elizabethan culture

- **Sir Francis Drake** helped defeat the Spanish Armada; his ship the **Golden Hind** sailed around the world.
- English settlement on the east coast of America began during Elizabeth's reign.
- The Elizabethan age is famous for poetry and drama.

### William Shakespeare

- **William Shakespeare** was born in Stratford-upon-Avon.
- Famous plays include *A Midsummer Night's Dream*, *Hamlet*, *Macbeth*, and *Romeo and Juliet*.

### The King James Bible

- In **1603**, **James VI of Scotland** became **James I of England**.
- He wanted a single authorised Bible; the **King James Bible** was completed in **1611**.

### The Civil War

- **Charles I** believed in the **Divine Right of Kings** and tried to raise taxes without Parliament's approval.
- This led to the **English Civil War** (1642-1649).
- **Oliver Cromwell** created the **New Model Army**.
- Parliament won; Charles I was executed in **1649**.
- England became a **republic** called the **Commonwealth**.

### The Restoration

- **Charles II** was invited back from exile; this is known as the **Restoration**.
- He supported the **Royal Society**; its first president was **Sir Christopher Wren**.
- Wren designed many new buildings in London after the Great Fire of **1666**, including St Paul's Cathedral.

### The Glorious Revolution

- **James II** was Catholic; he wanted more tolerance for Catholics.
- Important Protestants invited **William of Orange** to England in **1688**.
- James II left without fighting; this is called the **Glorious Revolution**.
- **William and Mary** jointly accepted the throne.
- James's supporters were known as **Jacobites**.

# A modern, thriving society

## 9. The UK today

### Diversity in modern UK society

- The UK is more ethnically and religiously diverse than it was a century ago.
- Nearly **10%** of people have at least one parent or grandparent born outside the UK.

### The nations of the UK

- The UK is in **north-west Europe**.
- The longest mainland distance is from **John O'Groats** to **Land's End** (about **870 miles**).
- Most people live in towns and cities.

### Capital cities

| Nation / area | Capital city |
|---|---|
| UK | **London** |
| Scotland | **Edinburgh** |
| Wales | **Cardiff** |
| Northern Ireland | **Belfast** |

### Major UK cities

- **England**: London, Birmingham, Liverpool, Leeds, Sheffield, Bristol, Manchester, Bradford, Newcastle upon Tyne, Plymouth, Southampton, Norwich
- **Wales**: Cardiff, Swansea, Newport
- **Northern Ireland**: Belfast
- **Scotland**: Edinburgh, Glasgow, Dundee, Aberdeen

### Languages

- English is the main language.
- Other languages include Welsh, Scottish Gaelic, Irish Gaelic, and Scots.

### UK currency

- The UK currency is the **pound sterling** (£).
- **100 pence = £1**

### Population growth and ageing

- The UK population is growing and ageing.
- People are living longer; the number of older people is increasing.
- This affects healthcare, pensions, and social services.

### Religion

- The UK has a Christian history but is religiously diverse today.
- People have the legal right to practise a religion or have no religion.
- It is illegal to discriminate against someone because of their religion or belief.

### Equality and discrimination

- Men and women have equal rights.
- It is illegal to discriminate against someone because of their:
  - age
  - sex
  - race
  - religion
  - disability
  - sexual orientation
  - gender reassignment
  - marriage or civil partnership
  - pregnancy or maternity

### The UK population and census

- The census collects information about the population every **10 years**.
- It helps the government plan public services.
- The first census in England, Scotland, and Wales was in **1801**.
- Censuses are now held in the year ending in **1**.

## 10. Customs and traditions

### Main Christian festivals

#### Christmas

- **Christmas Day** is on **25 December**.
- It celebrates the birth of **Jesus Christ**.
- It is a public holiday.
- Many Christians attend church on Christmas Eve or Christmas Day.
- Common Christmas traditions include:
  - spending time at home with family
  - eating a special meal, often including turkey
  - Christmas pudding and mince pies
  - giving presents
  - sending cards
  - decorating homes and Christmas trees
- Young children often associate Christmas with **Father Christmas** (**Santa Claus**.

#### Boxing Day

- **Boxing Day** is **26 December**): the day after Christmas.
- It is a public holiday.

#### Easter

- **Easter** is in **March or April**.
- It marks the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ.
- **Good Friday** remembers the crucifixion.
- **Easter Sunday** celebrates Jesus rising from the dead.
- **Easter Monday** is the Monday after Easter Sunday.
- Good Friday and Easter Monday are public holidays.

#### Lent, Shrove Tuesday, and Ash Wednesday

- **Lent** is the 40-day period before Easter.
- Christians use Lent for reflection and preparation.
- Some people give up a favourite food or habit during Lent.
- **Shrove Tuesday** (**Pancake Day**): is the day before Lent starts.
- Pancakes were traditionally made to use up rich foods before fasting.
- **Ash Wednesday** is the first day of Lent.
- Some Christians attend services where ash is placed on the forehead as a sign of repentance and mortality.

- **Easter eggs**: Chocolate Easter eggs are often given as gifts.; They symbolise new life.


### Other religious festivals

| Festival | Main community | Key point |
|---|---|---|
| **Diwali** | Hindus and Sikhs | Festival of Lights; celebrates good overcoming evil and the gaining of knowledge |
| **Hannukah** | Jewish community | Eight-day festival remembering the struggle for religious freedom |
| **Eid al-Fitr** | Muslims | Marks the end of Ramadan, the month of fasting |
| **Eid ul Adha** | Muslims | Remembers Ibrahim’s willingness to sacrifice his son in obedience to God |
| **Vaisakhi / Baisakhi** | Sikhs | Celebrates the founding of the Khalsa |

### Festival details to remember

- **Diwali** usually falls in **October or November** and lasts **five days**.
- Leicester has a well-known Diwali celebration.
- **Hannukah** is usually in **November or December** and lasts **eight days**.
- During Hannukah, candles are lit on a menorah.
- **Eid al-Fitr** changes date each year because it follows the Islamic calendar.
- Muslims often attend special services and meals for Eid.
- **Eid ul Adha** is linked to sacrifice and commitment to God.
- Animal sacrifice for Eid ul Adha in Britain must be done in a slaughterhouse.
- **Vaisakhi** is celebrated on **14 April** with parades, music, dancing, and singing.

### Other festivals and traditions

# The UK government, the law and your role

## 16. Fundamental principles

### Human rights background

- Britain has a long tradition of protecting individual rights and basic freedoms.
- Important historical foundations include:
  - **Magna Carta**
  - **Habeas Corpus Act**
  - **Bill of Rights 1689**
- British lawyers and diplomats helped draft the **European Convention on Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms**.
- The UK was one of the first countries to sign the European Convention, in **1950**.
- The **Human Rights Act 1998** brought the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law.
- Government, public bodies, and courts must follow the Convention’s principles.

### Rights and freedoms to remember

The European Convention includes principles such as:

- right to life
- protection from torture
- protection from slavery and forced labour
- right to liberty and personal security
- right to a fair trial
- freedom of thought, conscience, and religion
- freedom of expression

### Equal opportunities

UK law protects people from unfair treatment in work and public life because of:

- age
- disability
- sex
- pregnancy and maternity
- race
- religion or belief
- sexuality
- marital status

If someone faces discrimination, support and advice may be available from organisations such as **Citizens Advice** and equality or human rights bodies in England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.

### Domestic violence

- Violence or abuse in the home is treated as a serious crime in the UK.
- A person can be prosecuted for violence against a partner, whether they are married, unmarried, living together, male, or female.
- Rape within marriage is a criminal offence.
- People experiencing domestic violence should seek help quickly.
- Help may be available from:
  - the police, especially in an emergency
  - Citizens Advice
  - solicitors
  - refuges or shelters
  - domestic abuse support organisations

### Female genital mutilation

- **Female genital mutilation (FGM)** is illegal in the UK.
- It is also illegal to take a girl or woman abroad for FGM.
- FGM is a criminal offence.

### Forced marriage

- Marriage must involve the full and free agreement of both people.
- **Arranged marriage** is legal if both people freely agree.
- **Forced marriage** is illegal if one or both people do not consent or cannot consent.
- Forcing someone to marry is a criminal offence.
- Courts can issue **Forced Marriage Protection Orders** to protect people.
- These orders were introduced:
  - in **2008** for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland
  - in **2011** for Scotland
- Breaching a protection order can lead to imprisonment.

### Income tax

People in the UK pay income tax on certain types of income, including:

- wages from employment
- profits from self-employment
- taxable benefits
- pensions
- income from property, savings, and dividends

Income tax helps pay for public services such as:

- roads
- education
- police
- armed forces

### PAYE and self-assessment

- For many employees, income tax is taken automatically from wages by the employer.
- This system is called **Pay As You Earn (PAYE)**.
- Employers send the tax to **HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC)**.
- **HMRC** is the government department responsible for collecting taxes.
- Self-employed people usually pay tax through **self-assessment**.
- Self-assessment normally means completing a tax return.
- Some other people may also need to complete a tax return.
- If HMRC sends a tax return, it should be completed and returned when the necessary information is available.

### National Insurance

- Most people in paid work, including self-employed people, must pay **National Insurance Contributions**.
- National Insurance helps fund state benefits and services, including:
  - State Pension
  - National Health Service (NHS)
- Employees normally have National Insurance deducted from pay by their employer.
- Self-employed people pay National Insurance themselves.
- Not paying enough National Insurance may affect entitlement to some benefits or a full State Pension.
- Some workers may not qualify for certain statutory payments if their earnings are too low.

### National Insurance number

- A **National Insurance number** is a personal account number.
- It helps make sure tax and National Insurance payments are recorded correctly.
- Young people in the UK normally receive one before their **16th birthday**.
- A non-UK national who is living in the UK and working, looking for work, or becoming self-employed usually needs one.
- A person may start work without one if they can prove they have the right to work in the UK.
- Applications are made online and require identity checks.

### Driving in the UK

- You must usually be at least **17** to drive a car or motorcycle on public roads.
- You need a valid driving licence.
- To get a UK driving licence, you must pass a test covering:
  - knowledge
  - practical driving ability
- You must be at least **16** to ride a moped.
- Larger vehicles have extra age rules and tests.
- Driving licences are valid until age **70**.
- After age 70, licences are renewed for **three years** at a time.
- In **Northern Ireland**, new drivers must display an **R plate** for one year after passing the driving test.
- A licence from another country may usually be used for up to **12 months**, but rules should be checked.

### Vehicle rules

- UK residents must register cars or motorcycles with the **Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency (DVLA)**.
- Vehicle tax must be paid annually.
- Vehicle tax does not transfer automatically when a vehicle changes owner.
- If a vehicle is kept off-road and not used, the owner must tell DVLA using a **Statutory Off Road Notification (SORN)**.
- A SORN does not transfer if the vehicle is sold or given away.
- Drivers must have valid motor insurance.
- Driving without insurance is a serious criminal offence.
- Vehicles over **three years old** must normally have an annual **MOT** test.
- It is an offence to use a vehicle over three years old without a valid MOT certificate.

### Key things to remember



- Magna Carta, Habeas Corpus, and the Bill of Rights helped shape UK rights.
- The UK signed the European Convention on Human Rights in 1950.
- The Human Rights Act 1998 made the Convention part of UK law.
- UK law protects people from discrimination.
- Domestic violence, FGM, and forced marriage are illegal.
- Arranged marriage is legal only when both people freely agree.

## 19. Respecting the law

### Why respecting the law matters

- Obeying the law is one of the main responsibilities of UK residents.
- Some actions that may be accepted in other countries are illegal or unacceptable in the UK.
- People who do not respect UK law may harm their chances of becoming permanent residents.
- UK law affects everyday life, including:
  - personal behaviour
  - family matters
  - housing
  - work
  - business
  - public conduct

### Equal treatment under the law: Everyone in the UK is treated equally under the law.; The same legal rules apply to all people, regardless of who they are or where they come from..

### Criminal law and civil law

| Type of law | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Criminal law** | Deals with crimes. These are usually investigated by the police or another authority and punished by courts. |
| **Civil law** | Deals with disagreements between people, organisations, or groups. |

### Examples of criminal law

Criminal offences include:

- **Carrying weapons** — it is illegal to carry weapons, including for self-defence. This includes guns, knives, and items made or adapted to injure people.
- **Illegal drugs** — buying or selling drugs such as heroin, cocaine, ecstasy, and cannabis is illegal.
- **Racial or religious harassment** — causing alarm, distress, or harassment because of someone’s religion or ethnic background is a criminal offence.
- **Selling tobacco to under-18s** — tobacco products must not be sold to anyone under **18**.
- **Smoking in enclosed public places** — smoking tobacco is banned in nearly all enclosed public places; signs show where smoking is not allowed.
- **Alcohol sales and buying** — it is illegal to sell alcohol to under-18s or buy alcohol for under-18s.
- **Drinking in public** — some areas are alcohol-free zones, and police may confiscate alcohol or move people on. Fines or arrest may apply.

Other crimes, such as murder, theft, and assault, are also part of criminal law.

### Alcohol age exception: People aged **16 or over** may drink alcohol with a meal in a hotel or restaurant.; This exception does not mean they can buy alcohol themselves..

### Examples of civil law

Civil law includes disputes such as:

- **Housing disputes** — for example, problems between landlords and tenants over repairs or eviction.
- **Consumer rights** — for example, disputes about faulty goods or poor services.
- **Employment disputes** — for example, wages, unfair dismissal, or workplace discrimination.
- **Debt** — people may be taken to court if they owe money.

### Police duties

The police in the UK are responsible for:

- protecting life and property
- keeping public order (keeping the peace
- preventing crime
- detecting crime

### Police organisation

- The police are organised into separate police forces.
- Each force is led by a **Chief Constable**.
- Police forces are independent of the government.
- In England and Wales): **Police and Crime Commissioners (PCCs)** were first elected in **November 2012**.
- PCCs are directly elected and help ensure local policing reflects community needs.
- PCC responsibilities include:
  - setting local police priorities
  - setting the local policing budget
  - appointing the local Chief Constable

### Police standards

- Police officers must obey the law.
- They must not:
  - misuse their authority
  - make false statements
  - act rudely or abusively
  - discriminate on racial grounds
- Corruption or misuse of police authority can lead to serious punishment.

### Police Community Support Officers

- Police officers may be supported by **Police Community Support Officers (PCSOs)**.
- PCSO duties vary by area.
- They often:
  - patrol streets
  - work with the public
  - support police at crime scenes
  - support police at major events

### Helping the police

- People in the UK are expected to help the police prevent and detect crime where possible.
- If arrested and taken to a police station:
  - the police must explain the reason for the arrest
  - the person can seek legal advice

### Complaints about the police

- Anyone can complain about the police.
- Complaints can be made:
  - at a police station
  - by writing to the Chief Constable of the relevant police force
  - through independent complaint bodies in England, Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland

### Terrorism and extremism

- The UK faces threats from terrorism.
- The topic mentions threats linked to:
  - Al Qa’ida and similar organisations
  - Northern Ireland-related terrorism
  - other forms of extremism
- Terrorist groups may try to radicalise or recruit people.
- Extremism means vocal or active opposition to fundamental British values.
- Extremism can include:
  - religious extremism
  - far-right extremism
- People should feel safe from all forms of extremism.
- If someone tries to persuade you to join an extremist or terrorist cause, you should contact your local police.

### Key things to remember



- All UK residents must know and obey the law.
- The law applies equally to everyone.
- Criminal law deals with crimes; civil law deals with disputes.
- Carrying weapons, drug dealing, racial harassment, selling tobacco to under-18s, and many alcohol offences are criminal matters.
- Civil law includes housing, consumer, employment, and debt disputes.
- Police protect life and property, keep the peace, and prevent and detect crime.

## 20. The British constitution

### What is the constitution?

- The UK constitution is not contained in one single written document.
- It is made up of:
  - **statute law** (laws passed by Parliament)
  - **common law** (laws developed through court decisions)
  - **conventions** (traditions and practices)
  - **authoritative works** (books by experts)

### Constitutional monarchy

- The UK is a **constitutional monarchy**.
- The monarch is the head of state but does not run the government.
- The monarch's role is mainly ceremonial.
- The monarch opens parliamentary sessions, gives royal assent to laws, and meets the Prime Minister regularly.
- The monarch is also the head of the Commonwealth.

### The Royal Family

- The monarch's role is to represent the UK at home and abroad.
- The monarch also supports charities and good causes.
- The monarch meets the Prime Minister every week.
- The monarch gives honours, such as **knighthoods**, to people who have made important contributions.
- The monarch's birthday is celebrated officially as the **King's Birthday** (or Queen's Birthday).
- This is celebrated in June, not on the monarch's actual birthday.
- There is a parade called **Trooping the Colour**.

### The monarch's ceremonial duties

- The monarch represents the UK at important public events.
- The monarch visits other countries and hosts foreign leaders.
- The monarch awards honours and medals.
- The monarch supports many charities and organisations as a patron.

### The role of the Prime Minister

- The **Prime Minister** is the head of the UK government.
- The Prime Minister is the leader of the party with the most MPs.
- The Prime Minister:
  - appoints ministers
  - chairs Cabinet meetings
  - represents the UK abroad
- The Prime Minister lives at **10 Downing Street**.

### The Cabinet

- The **Cabinet** is made up of senior ministers.
- Cabinet meetings are usually held every week.
- Ministers must support government decisions in public (**collective responsibility**).

### The Opposition

- The **Opposition** challenges government policy and suggests alternatives.
- The **Leader of the Opposition** questions the Prime Minister at **Prime Minister's Questions (PMQs)** every Wednesday.

### The Speaker

- The **Speaker** chairs debates in the House of Commons and ensures fair debate.
- The Speaker is an MP but does not take part in party politics.

## 21. The development of British democracy

### What democracy means

- Democracy means the adult population has a say in how the country is governed.
- People may take part by:
  - voting directly on issues
  - choosing representatives to make decisions for them
- In the UK, voters elect representatives, especially Members of Parliament.

### Britain before modern democracy

- At the start of the **19th century**, Britain was not a democracy in the modern sense.
- Elections existed, but only a small group could vote.
- Voting was mainly limited to men who:
  - were over **21**
  - owned enough property
- Most ordinary men and all women were excluded from voting.

### Expansion of the franchise

- **Franchise** means the right to vote, or the number of people allowed to vote.
- During the **19th century**, the franchise expanded.
- Political parties began to involve more ordinary men and women as members.
- Voting rights widened gradually rather than through one single sudden change.

### Chartists

- The **Chartists** were a reform movement active in the **1830s and 1840s**.
- They campaigned to make the political system more democratic.
- Their demands included:
  - votes for all men
  - yearly elections
  - fairer representation across all regions
  - secret ballots
  - the right for any man to stand as an MP
  - payment for MPs

### Results of Chartist ideas

- At the time, the Chartist campaign was largely seen as unsuccessful.
- However, by **1918**, most of their reforms had been introduced.
- Their ideas helped shape later democratic change.

### Votes for women and equal voting age

- ****1918****: voting rights were extended to women over **30**.
- ****1928****: men and women over **21** gained voting rights on the same terms.
- ****1969****: the voting age was lowered to **18** for both men and women.

### Quick timeline

| Date / period | What to remember |
|---|---|
| Start of 19th century | Only a small group of property-owning men over 21 could vote |
| 1830s–1840s | Chartists campaigned for democratic reform |
| 1918 | Most Chartist reforms had been adopted; women over 30 gained the vote |
| 1928 | Men and women over 21 gained equal voting rights |
| 1969 | Voting age reduced to 18 for men and women |

### Key things to remember



- The UK is a parliamentary democracy with the monarch as head of state.
- Democracy gives adults a say in government, usually through elected representatives.
- At the start of the 19th century, only some property-owning men over 21 could vote.
- Franchise means the right to vote.
- The Chartists campaigned for wider voting rights and political reform.
- By 1918, most Chartist demands had become law.

## 22. The government

### The UK Parliament

- Parliament sits in the **Palace of Westminster**.
- It is made up of the monarch, the **House of Commons**, and the **House of Lords**.
- The monarch's role is mainly ceremonial (opens sessions, gives royal assent).
- The **House of Commons** is the main law-making body with **650 MPs**.
- Each MP represents a **constituency** and holds local **surgeries**.
- The **House of Lords** includes hereditary peers and life peers.
- The Lords can suggest changes and delay some laws, but cannot block money bills.

### The party system

- Main parties: **Conservative**, **Labour**, **Liberal Democrats**.
- Regional parties: **SNP** (Scotland), **Plaid Cymru** (Wales).
- The party with the most MPs forms the government; the second largest forms the **Opposition**.
- The **Leader of the Opposition** leads the **Shadow Cabinet**.

### The civil service

- The **civil service** supports the government and is politically neutral.
- Ministers make policy; civil servants implement it.

### Local government

- Local authorities run services including education, social services, rubbish collection, and road maintenance.
- They are funded by central government and **Council Tax** (paid by property owners/renters).

### The devolved administrations

- Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland have devolved governments (Scottish Parliament, Welsh Senedd, Northern Ireland Assembly).
- They control education, health, and transport.
- The UK Parliament controls defence, foreign policy, and immigration.

### The Prime Minister and Cabinet

- The **Prime Minister** is the head of government, leader of the party with the most MPs.
- The PM appoints ministers, chairs Cabinet meetings, and lives at **10 Downing Street**.
- The **Cabinet** is made up of senior ministers.
- Ministers must support government decisions in public (**collective responsibility**).

### The Opposition

- The **Opposition** challenges government policy and suggests alternatives.
- The **Leader of the Opposition** questions the Prime Minister at **Prime Minister's Questions (PMQs)** every Wednesday.

### The Speaker

- The **Speaker** chairs debates in the House of Commons and ensures fair debate.
- The Speaker is an MP but does not take part in party politics.

### How elections work

- MPs are elected at a **general election**, which must be held at least every **5 years**.
- The Prime Minister can ask the monarch to call an earlier election.
- Voting is by secret ballot.
- The candidate with the most votes in each constituency becomes the MP.
- This system is called **first past the post**.
- Voting age is **18**.
- To vote, you must be on the **electoral register**.

### Voting systems for devolved governments and local elections

- The Scottish Parliament, Welsh Parliament, and Northern Ireland Assembly use different voting systems.
- **Additional Member System (AMS)**: voters choose a constituency MP and a party list.
- **Single Transferable Vote (STV)**: voters rank candidates in order of preference.
- Local elections in England and Wales usually use **first past the post**.
- Some areas use the **Supplementary Vote (SV)** system for electing mayors.

### How to vote

- You can vote in person at a polling station, by post, or by proxy.
- Polling stations are open from **7am to 10pm** on election day.
- You will be given a ballot paper with a list of candidates.
- Mark an **X** next to your chosen candidate.
- Fold the paper and put it in the ballot box.

## 23. The role of the courts

### Judiciary

- Judges are collectively known as the **judiciary**.
- Their main roles are to:
  - interpret the law
  - make sure trials are fair
  - decide legal disputes
  - protect legal rights
- The government must not interfere with judges.
- If a court decides that government action is unlawful, the government may need to:
  - change the policy
  - ask Parliament to change the law
- If a public body breaches someone’s legal rights, judges can order it to:
  - change its behaviour
  - pay compensation
- Judges also decide disputes involving:
  - contracts
  - property
  - employment rights
  - accidents and compensation

### Criminal courts

Court systems differ slightly across England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.

#### Minor criminal cases

| Area | Court |
|---|---|
| England, Wales, Northern Ireland | **Magistrates’ Court** |
| Scotland | **Justice of the Peace Court** |

- Magistrates and Justices of the Peace are usually local community members.
- In England, Wales, and Scotland, they are usually unpaid.
- They do not normally need legal qualifications.
- They receive training and are supported by a legal adviser.
- They decide whether the defendant is guilty and, if so, the sentence.
- In Northern Ireland, minor cases are heard by a legally qualified paid District Judge or Deputy District Judge.

#### Serious criminal cases

| Area | Court |
|---|---|
| England, Wales, Northern Ireland | **Crown Court** |
| Scotland | **Sheriff Court** |
| Scotland, most serious cases such as murder | **High Court** |

- Serious cases in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland are tried before a judge and jury.
- Serious Scottish cases may be heard by a sheriff alone or by a sheriff with a jury.
- The most serious Scottish cases are heard by a judge and jury in the High Court.
- The **Old Bailey** is a famous criminal court in London.

### Juries

- Juries are made up of members of the public chosen randomly from the local electoral register.
- Jury sizes:
  - **12 people** in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland
  - **15 people** in Scotland
- People called for jury service must usually attend unless:
  - they are not eligible, for example due to a criminal conviction
  - they have a valid reason to be excused, such as serious illness
- The jury listens to the evidence and decides the verdict.
- Possible verdicts:
  - **guilty**
  - **not guilty**
  - **not proven** in Scotland only
- If the jury finds the defendant guilty, the judge decides the penalty.

### Youth courts and young offenders

- In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, most cases involving accused people aged **10 to 17** are heard in a **Youth Court**.
- Youth Court cases are usually heard by:
  - up to three specially trained magistrates
  - or a District Judge
- Very serious youth cases can go to the Crown Court.
- Parents or carers are expected to attend Youth Court hearings.
- Youth Courts are not open to the general public.
- The young person’s name or photograph cannot normally be published.
- Scotland uses the **Children’s Hearings System** for children and young people who offend.
- Northern Ireland uses **youth conferencing** to decide how young offenders should be dealt with.

### Civil courts

Civil courts deal with non-criminal disputes.

#### County Courts and Sheriff Courts

- **County Courts** handle many civil disputes in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland.
- Examples include:
  - money owed
  - personal injury
  - family matters
  - contract disputes
  - divorce
- In Scotland, many of these matters are dealt with in the **Sheriff Court**.
- More serious civil cases are heard in:
  - the **High Court** in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland
  - the **Court of Session** in Edinburgh for Scotland

### Small claims

- The small claims process is designed for minor disputes.
- It is intended to be simpler, cheaper, and less formal than full court proceedings.
- The hearing is usually in an ordinary room before a judge.
- People from both sides sit around a table.
- Small claims limits:
  - England and Wales: under **£10,000**
  - Scotland: **£5,000** under the simple procedure
  - Northern Ireland: **£5,000**
- In England and Wales, small claims can also be started online through Money Claims Online.

### Legal advice and solicitors

- **Solicitors** are trained lawyers.
- They can:
  - give legal advice
  - act for clients
  - represent clients in court
- Solicitors often specialise in particular areas of law.
- Before using a solicitor, it is important to check:
  - their area of expertise
  - whether they have suitable experience
  - likely costs
- Solicitors’ fees are often based on the time spent on a case.
- Advice about finding solicitors may be available from:
  - Citizens Advice
  - Law Society organisations in England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland

### Key things to remember



- Judges are independent from government.
- The judiciary interprets law and ensures fair trials.
- Courts can require government or public bodies to change unlawful actions.
- Minor criminal cases go to Magistrates’ Courts, or Justice of the Peace Courts in Scotland.
- Serious criminal cases go to Crown Courts, Sheriff Courts, or the High Court in Scotland.
- Juries have 12 members in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, and 15 in Scotland.

## 24. The UK and international institutions

### The Commonwealth

- The **Commonwealth** is a voluntary association of countries.
- Most members were once part of the British Empire.
- A small number of members did not have that historical link but joined later.
- Member countries work together on shared aims, especially:
  - democracy
  - development
  - good government
  - rule of law
- **King Charles III** is the ceremonial head of the Commonwealth.
- The Commonwealth has **56 member states** in this topic.
- It does not govern its members.
- It can suspend a country’s membership.

### Council of Europe

- The **Council of Europe** includes the UK.
- It has **46 member countries** in this topic.
- Its main focus is protecting and promoting human rights.
- It cannot make laws for member countries.
- It creates conventions and charters.
- Its best-known agreement is the **European Convention on Human Rights**.

### United Nations

- The UK is a member of the **United Nations (UN)**.
- The UN has more than **190 member countries**.
- It was created after the Second World War.
- Its aims include:
  - preventing war
  - promoting international peace
  - supporting international security
- The **UN Security Council** has **15 members**.
- The Security Council recommends action during international crises or threats to peace.
- The UK is one of the **five permanent members** of the Security Council.

### NATO

- NATO stands for the **North Atlantic Treaty Organization**.
- It includes countries from Europe and North America.
- NATO members agree to help each other if attacked.
- NATO also aims to maintain peace among its members.
